The Defining Moment

The Million-Dollar Check

"I had to take courage deep down, as deep as you go, to find the courage to rip that check up."

Tim was drowning in debt. A quarter million dollars at eighteen percent interest. A four-year-old and two-year-old at home. His boss offered him a blank check, anything under a million dollars.

For twenty minutes, Tim stared at that check. It would eliminate his debt, secure his family's future, buy them a house. But it came with invisible chains.

"That's not the way I'm going to earn my money. I'm going to earn it with someone, not for someone."

The Choice

That torn check became the foundation of a four billion dollar empire. Sometimes your greatest moment of courage looks like your biggest mistake.

The Man Behind the Story

From Valet to Visionary

"I changed my name in third grade from John to Tim. That was the first thing I remember doing to sort of recreate who I was." From that early act of self-determination to building a global empire, Tim's story is about the courage to reinvent yourself.

Founder of Outback Steakhouse, five-time United States Open Polo Champion, and world number one player at age fifty. But the real story is not the achievements, it is the principles that made them inevitable.

"Dreams do not expire. They just wait for you to find the courage to pursue them."

The Philosophy

"When you don't have a father, mothers teach you right from wrong. But fathers teach you how to make money."

Tim learned to find mentors everywhere and turn every setback into a setup for something greater.

Humble Beginnings

Raised by a single mother alongside five siblings in Fort Lauderdale, Tim learned early that success required hustle. While other kids played sports, he sold newspapers from age ten and parked cars at the Mai Kai restaurant through high school.

"My mother raised six children without a father. I never had that throw the ball in the backyard type of experience. So I had to find my mentors everywhere I went."

Finding Your Velcro

At sixteen, Tim's best friend's father told him about three ways to make enough money to play polo. The third way? "Like George de Mestral, the Swiss watchmaker who pulled sand spurs off his dog's hair and invented Velcro. Find something unique that the marketplace needs."

The Bloomin' Onion became Tim's Velcro.

The Velcro Moment

Inspired by a Japanese cookbook and New Orleans street food, Tim created the Bloomin' Onion in the mid-1980s. But sourcing the perfect onion proved impossible until he flew to Texas A&M and convinced Dr. Leonard Pike to give him seeds for a custom onion variety.

He then persuaded Idaho and Oregon farmers to grow these massive, single-heart onions. "I told them I would make onions famous one day," Tim recalls.

Today, the Bloomin' Onion has generated over $1.2 billion in sales and remains the #1 selling appetizer in casual dining for 35 years.

Leadership Philosophy

People First

"It was not about making ourselves rich. It was about spreading the wealth. Our manager partners wrote $25,000 checks to work for us and made $200,000 running one restaurant five nights a week."

Team Building

"I do not think I am a great polo player, but I am a great team builder. Find people with different skill sets that complement yours. When you meet talented people, stay in touch and collaborate."

Send the Elevator Back Down

"As you're going up, remember the people below you. I come from a family of social workers who do good but don't make money. I found a way to do both—and help them all along the way."
